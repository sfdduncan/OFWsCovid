Exploration of Overseas Filipino Workers

Jane Cole, Steven Duncan, Sojung Kim, Benedetta von Palombini

Background Info

Overseas Filipino Workers (OFW)—migrants with Filipino citizenship temporarily employed abroad—are major contributors to the nation’s economy and sociopolitical landscape. With over two million OFW employed annually, the Philippines is the highest per capita exporter of labor in Southeast Asia, and the ninth highest globally; further, the Filipino diaspora of migrant labor is among the largest globally (Migration Data Portal, 2023). Though often rightfully touted by the Philippine government as “bagong bayani” (“modern heroes”) due to their significant contributions to their families and national economy, OFW have reported facing myriad challenges working abroad, including but not limited to racial discrimination and abuse, financial instability, and social isolation (Atos et. al., 2022).

In 2020, the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic both exacerbated these precarious, trying conditions in which OFW lived, as well as posed a new challenge; thousands of OFW contracts were suddenly terminated, prompting a mass repatriation of Filipinos that ultimately resulted in a 75% reduction of OFW deployment (International Organization for Migration, 2021). This inevitably left both the Philippine economy (the Southeast Asian country most dependent on remittances) and millions of Filipino families extremely vulnerable.

This project attempts to provide insights into how migration patterns, demographics, and employment conditions among OFW shifted as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic. The findings are based on data reported by the Philippine Statistics Authority, though of course many OFW remain uncounted. Thus, while this project does not claim to provide comprehensive data, it aims to illuminate the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on broad trends among Filipino workers abroad, as well as on individual OFW lived experiences.

OFW During the COVID-19 Pandemic

OFW’s employment and living conditions are affected by a range of factors: government policies on migrants, hiring processes, the sociocultural conditions of their host environment, and from 2020 to 2022, the impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic (e.g. Au et al. 2019; Kaur-Gill,Qin-Liang, and Hassan 2021). Being non-residential workers, OFW are often excluded from the protection of local policies, and their comparatively low-paid income and social status aggravate their vulnerable circumstances in their places of work.

According to a study conducted by International Organization for Migration of the UN, of 8,332 OFW interviewed who were repatriated during the pandemic (just over 1% of the total OWF who returned to the Philippines between March and December 2020) 45% did so because their contracts ended and did not get renewed due to the pandemic. Furthermore, 73% of OFW reported that they did not receive support from their employer before leaving their host country to return to the Philippines. And not surprisingly, women were less likely than men to receive any kind of assistance from their employers. 17% surveyed also stated that they had not received their final salary prior to returning home (International Organization for Migration, 2021).

Despite this lack of support—and other well-documented hardships faced by OFW both before and during the pandemic—nearly half (48%) of those surveyed expressed their intention to return to work abroad once accessible. This further emphasizes the degree to which the nation and its citizens are economically dependent on remittances; 83% of OFW remained unemployed three months post arrival to the Philippines, and 78% reported finding a job or income-generating activity as their most pressing challenge (International Organization for Migration, 2021).

Number of Overseas Workers 2019

Prior to the COVID-19 pandemic, projections anticipated a steady rise in the number of Overseas Filipino Workers (OFWs) from the reported 2.2 million in 2019, reflecting the ongoing trend of increasing labor migration driven by improved employment opportunities abroad.

Overseas Workers in 2020 Mid Year

However, contrary to predictions, the number of OFWs sharply declined in 2020, falling to 1.77 million due to the pandemic. Global travel restrictions, economic downturns, and the suspension of overseas employment contracts left many workers stranded or forced to return home. This sudden decline disrupted their livelihoods and placed significant financial strain on their families, who rely heavily on remittances to meet daily needs.

Gender Breakdown

In 2020, the majority of Overseas Filipino Workers (OFWs) were women, comprising approximately 60% of the total workforce. Female OFWs were predominantly employed in domestic work, caregiving, and healthcare roles, reflecting the high demand for these professions globally, even amid the challenges posed by the COVID-19 pandemic.

Age Breakdown

The largest age groups among OFWs in 2020 was 30 to 39 years old, making up roughly 41% of the workforce. Younger workers, aged 25 to 29, accounted for a significant portion as well, indicating the continued trend of younger Filipinos seeking opportunities abroad despite the uncertainties caused by the pandemic..

Job Type

In 2020, service-related jobs, such as domestic work and caregiving, remained the most common employment sector for OFWs, representing nearly 58% of all jobs. Other sectors, including construction and manufacturing, saw a notable decline due to pandemic-related disruptions, which limited opportunities in these fields.

Cash Remittances

Overseas Filipino workers (OFWs) are hailed as active development agents by the Philippine government, and are often referred to as Bagong-Bayanis or modern-day heroes (Eugenio 2023). According to a survey published by the Social Weather Stations (SWS) , they found that 7 percent of Filipino households have an OFW who financially supports the family, and 75 percent of households frequently receive money from their OFW family member. Their economic contributions are well-recognized, and have been an important source of spurring consumer spending and boosting economic conditions of Filipino families. In turn, this has continued to support economic growth in the Philippines and enhance governance and human development in OFW’s home provinces (Eugenio 2023).

Cash remittances sent back to the Philippines have steadily grown over the decade, and have grown by almost 57% from 2012 to 2023. Although there was a reduction in remittances in 2020 due to COVID-19, there has been a steady incline in total remittances since then. Just in the last year alone, OFWs have sent PhP 238.63 billion (approx. $4 billion) back to the Philippines in cash remittances. The top countries that OFWs have been consecutively sending remittances from since 2019 include Singapore, Saudi Arabia, United Arab Emirates, Japan, Canada, United Kingdom, and the United States of America. Countries such as Qatar, Hong Kong, South Korea, Taiwan, Kuwait, and Germany are among those from which OFWs have sent remittances in various years since 2019 (Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas).

Given that OFWs often serve as the backbone of their families back in the Philippines, and contribute significantly to the country’s GDP, it is important to recognize the geopolitical landscape in which they are forced to navigate (Eugenio 2023). Many OFWs face injustices in their host countries, and often do not even receive the minimal level of protection and benefits. Additionally, COVID-19 has shed light on the precarious conditions of OFWs not only abroad but also the situations that forced them to work overseas in the first place (Borja 2021). While the globalization of oversea workers has presented opportunities for OFWs to support their families back at home, there remains a pressing need to continue to advocate for reform and to acknowledge the ongoing challenges of perseverance and emotional labor that OFWs have endured and continue to face (ibid).
